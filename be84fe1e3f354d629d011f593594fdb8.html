The News

Ugly data breach of 60 GP clinics & OAIC finds Qantas innocent

Partnered Health, the operator of around 60 doctor's clinics across Australia, had a nasty data breach. They posted to their website that the usual personal info was stolen, along with "medical information and treatment details, including consultation notes, referral letters, and pathology or diagnostic results recorded by a GP or other medical professionals at our clinics", by an as yet unknown hacker via an unknown method. Yikes. I'd be very upset, maybe even angry, if the notes between my GP and I were somehow made public. On a bigger, but less devastating scale, the OAIC made a statement about that Qantas breach last year saying that Qantas did nothing wrong, it was just bad luck someone at the overseas call centre got social engineered and that there's no need for a deeper investigation, nor any punishment for Qantas.

Europe tells Google to play fair with other AI apps on Android

The Europeans are forcing Google to give other AI companies the same access to Android and Google Search as Google has, due to the EU's Digital Markets Act (the law Apple keeps sooking about). This means apps like Claude and ChatGPT, in Europe at least, have to get the same access to Android devices features as what Google gives Gemini. For search, Google is already supposed to share its search data with other search engines, but the EC reckons they aren't sharing it with other AI chatbots, so they have to do that now, at the same level Gemini has access to Google Search. Google has to "start sharing search data with eligible search engine providers from January 2027" and "users will start benefitting from the changes to Android as of July 2027" - or else there's massive fines.

Canva's co-founder makes glorified HTML form for Wollongong

Canva and the City of Wollongong have launched the Wollongong Goals website where the fine folk of the Gong can submit "their goals for their community". There's no obligation for the council to do anything with this info, nor is there any commitment from Canva to give any money to realise these goals. Here's an interview with Melanie Perkins explaining it. The whole thing seems relatively benign, so if it helps turn some of Perkin's & Obrecht's immense wealth into little crumbs for the rest of us (their foundation does give away some money - $18m last year, the equivalent of the average Aussie millennial donating $776), and helps her sleep at night dealing with the guilt of getting obscenely rich off the backs of Canva's 5,500 employees, good luck to her and Wollongong. Maybe she'll give $50,000 to plant some trees, like how I give whatever coins are in my pocket to a homeless guy outside Southern Cross station.

Something I Saw On The Internet

A real world example of how SEO killed the world wide web

This article is a few years old now, but HouseFresh outlines how websites doing the right thing have been suffocated by low effort crap that's better at manipulating Google. The bargain we had with Google and the general internet public was that we let it scrape our sites and make the website available for free, Google rewards us with rivers of traffic. That hasn't been true for years as SEO took over and is even worse now that people are using AI instead of searching, because the SEO slop got so bad. What's the incentive to do the kind of work HouseFresh does when there's so little reward?
