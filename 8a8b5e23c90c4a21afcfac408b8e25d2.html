A Farewell to Code

There’s a chemical in your brain that makes you dread doing your taxes. Mine is amplified tenfold—call it bitaracitate. It’s debilitating around the wrong task and narcotic around the right one.

When it finds something it likes, it doesn’t let go.

For me, the opposite of taxes was coding.

I looked forward to unsealing my laptop every morning and summoning a code editor, like I looked forward to the morning’s first coffee. Together? Bliss.

You need not be a coder to understand: think of a time you published a blog post. Created a presentation. Completed a long form you’ve been putting off. The charge you got from that was enough to power your entire day. Three or four hours of this stuff and you felt like a god.

What was it about coding specifically?

It was the music of mentation. The percussion of keys as your fingers glided across the keyboard. The symphony of tension when a thing didn’t work, and the resolution when you thought your way through.

And the stakes. The way lines of code could be tracked to a spike in revenue. The satisfaction of making systems that instantly benefited thousands of users when deployed.

No craft moved faster from farm to table.

Today, the craft is unrecognizable.

Some people’s chemistry may find the new way satisfying. Mine does not.

Morning coffee-and-cogitation is replaced by morning coffee-and-wait. Watch the cursor blink while your alien contractor in Anthropic’s datacenter does your work. Read the alien’s work. Haggle until the result is to your lowered standard of satisfaction.

Even though the end result is the same—software delivered to customers—it’s an altogether different craft.

And it’s not the one I fell in love with, back when I began learning to code by borrowing PHP books from the library (the ones with a CD tucked in the back). Today newcomers largely do not need a book. The alien does everything.

If becoming an expert at your craft takes the proverbial 10,000 hours, then the best time to get started is young, so that by the time you’re twenty, you’ve basically gotten there for free.

My passion for computing had the lucky timing of arriving young, latching onto what would become one of the most lucrative skills of the 21st century.

Not that engineering is dead now—there will always be a need for the people keeping an eye on the aliens.

But I mourn the loss of my identity. The 10,000 hours that anyone can now rent for twenty dollars a month. Most days I sit here wondering what I still have to offer the world.

I’m grateful that I’ve had a second passion throughout: writing. It’s a close second in love, but a distant second in hours—maybe 3,500. Bitaracitate latches onto writing, but not with as satisfying a click as it did with coding.

I get a lot of YouTube comments from young people asking me what they should study in college.

And I think I know now what the best advice is:

Study more than one thing.
