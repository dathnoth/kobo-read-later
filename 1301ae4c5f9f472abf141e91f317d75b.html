A generational run

“Just track how much milk we’re putting her,” my wife said. “I put it on the calendar.”

“Why a calendar? Why not a note?” I asked.

“Because it’s shared. I’m tracking when I put her milk.”

“Like in her mouth or in her bottle?”

She looked at me dumbfounded. “In her bottle”—you idiot.

I sometimes make up fake questions to my wife’s housekeeping inquiries, of which there is seemingly a never-ending supply, to keep things interesting. My wife talks so fast that she’ll already be one or two words into her explanation before realizing it was a troll question, but continues to answer it anyway as if it weren’t.

When transactions like this happen, I try to think if there’s an app idea to be made here. Shared milk tracking app? Already exists. Shared couples productivity suite, like Google Workspace but for couples? Probably exists. But who needs it.

It’s been one year since I quit my job, and there’s hardly a day that goes by without me wondering if I made the right call. If it were any job, there probably wouldn’t be much to think about. But their company had acquired mine, and I still had two payments owed to me for the sale (I got one every year, up to three).

My pain function was such that I could not last in environments where my sole purpose was to secure a modest income and live life for its own sake. I was making $10k a month net, after taxes, and my expenses were also, somehow, $10k a month. (My county’s property taxes are insane. There really ought to be an investigation into this.)

And I got the itch.

The itch to do something else. Anything else. Sitting here breaking even? For what?

Worst of all, they had not given me a bonus on my twelfth month, and I had taken that very personally, considering the breakout year I had. My manager secretly hated me, though I figured not because of me personally, since he began hating me before we even met, but because of what I represented: the fast-paced shipping type who’s all execution, little planning. He was the opposite.

We got along courteously, and probably were it not for the circumstances, we might have even liked each other. But alas, we were both getting paid to be there.

I left one-and-a-half years into my three-year term. (I did hope this would be a forever-job).

It’s been exactly twelve months since I left, departing with a curt message in their #thanks channel, something like: Really great time here, time for me to try my hands at something new before my hairs gray any more than this. I thought it had the perfect blend of ambition, humility, and self-awareness. The twenty-year-old boy trapped inside a thirty-something’s body.

Like everything else I’d ever done, quitting was more execution than strategy.

At the very least I forbade myself to leave until I had something worth building. It took a few months to find, and when I found it, it was somehow convincing enough: what if I built a Slack alternative? Easy-pz, right? Slack was in need of disruption. The product had refused to evolve to how people work today. And it was a pain point I felt deeply, having used Slack for half my working hours while I was at the company.

When I think about building a Slack alternative today, I gag a little. I cannot believe I quit over that. What a retard. I mean, network effects, hello? Good lord.

But it gets worse. My timing was incredible.

A couple weeks after I quit, AI suddenly started getting really good at coding. At first this was a godsend. I was like oh hell yeah, right in time for me to build something extremely audacious. Can you imagine doing this by hand? But then the deal with the devil became apparent: when you used AI to work on something, it made work on it less interesting. It felt like managing a remote off-shore worker. You got the end-result done pretty quickly, satisfactorily even (but alas not as good as you might have done it yourself perhaps), but with very little emotional investment. You feel as little attachment to it as a step mother feels to her husband’s son.

That was the curse of AI. Use it to help you work → become less interested in the work (and lose the ability to work without it altogether.) A most devastating bargain. And yet, a deal that has been too sweet for most to pass up.

The virus hit me very early on, and I’ve not recovered. And probably never will.

I’ve been completely turned off to software development, much to my present dismay and to the dismay of the person who left his job twelve months ago to work on something more profitable than his breakeven wage labor.

In the interim was the death of one of the most profitable, lucrative industries to ever exist.

I’ve been trying to think of what else I can possibly work on, if not software. But really, you have to be world-class at something to compete. And the only thing I was world-class at was building apps.

What’s world-class’s little brother? State-class? I would say I’m state-class at writing. Non-fiction exclusively. Absolutely zero-class in fiction-writing. Could I turn this into a world-class affair?

Then what?

Write a novel I perhaps? Market it to my YouTube audience?

Ah, YouTube.

What was that all about?

I had risen to accidental YouTube fame about six months after quitting my job. It didn’t last long. I ended up sabotaging my runaway success. My pain function had.

While I was working on my Slack alternative, I thought it would be good to begin posting “dev logs” on YouTube, recording my screen and sharing what I was working on.

Eventually I grew disillusioned about using AI in development, realizing that agents had written really poor code when I had stopped to analyze the codebase as a whole. It was so bad that I had decided to go back to writing by hand. I made a video describing my experience. And it struck a nerve.

I had a few more bullets in the chamber and made more videos on the topic, things like There’s no skill in AI coding and Vibecoding will not make you an engineer, etc. The basic theme was that AI coding was a sham and it’s actually setting you up for failure. I still think this is the case, but no longer for technical reasons, but rather for the sole reason of it making projects uninteresting to work on.

I had been able to improvise talking points based on the finite set of experiences I had, but my ammunition would end up lasting no more than about a month and a half. I had the thought of beginning to make scripted videos that cover AI news humorously, sort of like The Onion, but presenting real facts.

And it worked.

It worked beyond my wildest dreams.

The first such video I made, discussing how Amazon was now surely regretting its decision to mainline AI after a series of “catastrophic” failures, went on to become my #1 video at the time, racking up 400,000 views in just a few days.

My prior videos had only gotten 30,000 views at best in a similar time window.

It was clear what the people wanted.

I continued making more videos in this vein and they did very well. The floor was somewhere around 260,000 views, and the ceiling was 1m views.

This worked well for about twenty videos. An incredible, generational run, if I may say so myself.

It seemed infinite. I can go on about this forever. Right?

But then it ran out.

I had exhausted the well of my personal philosophies about AI. More tragically, I had begun to care less about AI altogether, mostly born from a brief—what I will call “psychotic”—episode where I obsessed over the nature of consciousness for a couple-week period, and basically live-streamed the whole thing. You can imagine how that all went.

Not that it was awful or cringe. But you know, dealing with the world’s toughest question ruffled a few feathers. And the idea that I would figure it all out, the nature of intelligence, I mean, come on. What was I doing? It was like trying to prove the existence of God. It may be fine and well to believe God exists, but to be able to prove it, using words—oh man. The earlier you finish that phase of your life, the better.

So I noped the fuck out. I said, I’m done with this.

I also began to find it hypocritical to criticize AI as being a dumb autocomplete (which the fans absolutely roared over), and my reliance on it as an editor and guide through tough questions. It could be clumsy, but less and less so I thought, and highly dependent on what you asked of it. My experience with using it in a chat window was always pretty helpful and impressive.

It was tragic, really. I lost interest in the most lucrative message at the most lucrative time in history. Imagine if Jesus, one year into his impeccable run, just said fuck it, actually, I dunno.

Mostly the problem was that I lost my anchor into the reality of AI use. The best way to gauge its intelligence was to use it for coding, since conversational AI can be a bit of a rabbit hole, where you might lose sight of truth as the conversation went on. But truth was easy to ascertain when you’re using it to build an exacting product.

The problem was that I had nothing I wanted to build.

You will read this and feel it is a cute, light little, poetic, romantic problem. A person on a journey, or something. But I went to shed you of any such notion. It is in fact the ailment causing me the most amount of pain on a daily basis: I have no software product I have interest in building.

To make it worse, I’ve lost interest in being on my phone or using technology altogether. Not that I don’t—I do. But it’s just less interesting than it used to be. I find life to be more filling when the phone is at a safe distance away from you. Life swells with opportunity proportional to your phone’s distance.

I read more. A lot more. I feel like it’s good practice.

Practicing piano is painful. I don’t find it much fun. Which is why I haven’t been able to get too good. But a large part of practicing writing is reading. When I read, I feel like I’m growing. How much truth there is to this I don’t know. Probably some.

I’m looking for a north star.

A goal to set my sights upon. Something I can earn over the next 1–2 years and challenge myself towards. A way to feel useful. Ambitious. Productive. And the best I can find right now is learning the art of story-telling.

Fake stories, that is.

Real stories are easy enough: wait for something to happen then write about it. But it’s stubbornly finite.

I can’t help feeling the thing that clogged my previous efforts to make a sustainable YouTube channel has been my inability to make-believe. To imagine something beyond what exists.

Every time I try, I fail miserably. I get stuck and it gets repetitive quickly. The plot refuses to progress. The characters will talk and talk and talk and the story goes nowhere.

This is the coveted skill I seek right now. I had the idea to read a couple screenplays, particularly for the HBO show Silicon Valley. And the writing is so simple. So elemental. So lacking in anything magical. I could write this, I thought. Easily.

But it does the one thing I’m unable to do.

Yes I can probably master the mechanics. The sentence structure. The grammar.

But the ability to advance a plot. To make things happen.

I have no fucking idea how that’s done.
