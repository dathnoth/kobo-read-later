AI is not for you

So Peter Steinberger — the guy who invented OpenClaw, now works at OpenAI — posted a screenshot of his token consumption dashboard last week.

In the last seven days, Peter spent $250,000 on tokens.

In the last 30 days, he spent $1.3 million on tokens.

And before you get your pitchforks out, understand that Pete was doing a science experiment. It was a noble mission. He asked:

“How would we build software in the future if tokens didn’t matter?”

Which is just wonderful. It’s like a crack dealer asking, “How would society look if crack were free?”

Has that been tried? “How would we build houses if wood didn’t matter?” Would that solve the housing crisis? I keep hearing about this housing crisis. And these rocket ships Elon is sending into space — I mean, how much are these costing, dude? Just pretend like gravity didn’t matter. Problem solved, bro.

AI is here, and you will not be able to afford it. It’s not for you.

“But aren’t tokens getting cheaper?”

I know what you might be thinking. You’re probably thinking — well, aren’t tokens getting cheaper? Isn’t the trajectory going to be some kind of Moore’s law of diminishing costs until this stuff is basically free?

The answer to that is one of the biggest deceptions running in the industry right now. Not an intentional one. But a very sneaky one.

Because there have been two pricing charts.

There’s been one for the lower-end models. Those prices have been going down or staying the same, more or less. That’s the chart everyone tweets at you.

But they made a new chart — the chart for the model you actually have to use if you want to do any meaningful work. The price of those tokens has been going way up.

From GPT-5.4 to 5.5, the price of tokens doubled.

And with Anthropic’s Opus 4.7, the price stayed the same as 4.6 — but they introduced a new tokenizer that results in 35% longer responses. Same price per token. More tokens per response. Do the math.

OpenRouter did a study. They looked at what GPT-5.5 actually costs in the real world. The cost of GPT-5.5 compared to 5.4 was 49 to 92 percent higher.

So shed the notion in your mind that AI is going to get cheaper. That it’s going to be more affordable. That everyone will be able to do what Peter’s doing.

That doesn’t look like it’s going to be the case.

In fact, Mythos is so tantalizingly locked up that Andrej Karpathy just announced he’s joining Anthropic to get access to the model. He couldn’t figure out what was happening with Opus 4.7 from the outside. He felt left behind. He had to join the company.

Andrej Karpathy. The smartest, most beloved AI educator on the internet. Couldn’t access the frontier from the outside. Had to get a job at the lab to see what the lab was building.

At this rate, Anthropic and OpenAI are going to be the only employers.

Meanwhile, in Tucson

Eric Schmidt — former CEO of Google, net worth $60 billion — gave a commencement address at the University of Arizona.

He told the kids: “Say yes to the rocket ship.”

Every time he said the word “AI,” the crowd booed.

He’s saying — the rocket ship is AI. When someone tells you to get on the rocket ship, you don’t ask which seats.

The problem is, the rocket ship only has three seats. And you, Dario, and Sam have already occupied them.

The rocket ship has already taken off, and Eric is looking down out the window going “Get on, get on.”

And the kids are like — “Where, dude? There’s no room.”

He goes: “You’re not seeing the opportunity.”

And the rocket just takes off. The exhaust sets fire to the entire stadium. Eric writes on the window: “The future is unwritten.”

In reference to AI, he said in this speech:

“I know what many of you are feeling about it. I can hear you.”

Well, they’re not exactly whispering, Eric. They’re audibly booing you. This is not a subtle cue, bro.

He’s like — “I’m sensing something. There is a disturbance in the room.”

Yeah. The disturbance is twelve thousand people booing you.

There was an exact moment — you can see it on his face — where Eric realized he wrote the wrong speech for this occasion. He says the word AI for the first time, the booing starts, and he goes:

“Interesting. Interesting.”

That was the exact moment the bubble popped. Right there.

He was thinking: “I love AI. And I hate all of you. And they’re paying me a lot of money to be here. And none of you are going to get hired after this.”

And the kids boo, and he’s like — “Wait, what? Wait, you guys wanted jobs?”

So what do you do?

I’m trying to think — if you’re an eighteen-year-old kid right now, what do you learn? What do you practice?

You don’t have hundreds or thousands of dollars to spend a day. Do you do everything by hand?

If your goal is to make a little money, you need to iterate toward product-market fit as quickly as possible. And AI does help you throw darts. It can ship something really, really quickly and see if there’s market demand for your product before you invest all this time coding this up by hand.

My first app, decades ago, took me a full year to write. If I tried to do it now, it would take me half an hour. Literally.

And after that full year — the app failed. Because I didn’t know what I was doing. Now I could learn that lesson in an afternoon.

So someone who does have the money to spend on tokens will be able to iterate toward PMF way quicker than you.

That’s the actual bind. Not “AI will take your job.” Something subtler: AI will make the person who has the budget for it iterate so much faster than you that by the time you’ve shipped one product, they’ve shipped twenty and one of them worked.

The AI psychosis problem

Then there’s Mitchell Hashimoto. Founder of HashiCorp (acquired by IBM), creator of Ghostty — the terminal app a lot of people are using now.

What he’s saying is that a whole swath of companies have AI psychosis now as a whole. What these companies are doing is optimizing for fixing issues quicker rather than having fewer issues — because AI sucks at not creating issues. That’s what it does.

It’s like a bug machine. But it’s really good at fixing bugs very quickly.

Look no further than Anthropic, who leads the way in actually having a lot of bugs and taking a long time to solve them. So I don’t know where they lie on this spectrum, to be honest.

A lot of companies have a hypothesis for a feature that, in theory, should increase revenue. A lot of times they ship features that don’t improve the bottom line at all. So companies would prefer to ship something slightly buggy if they can do it in one-tenth of the time — and fix issues after.

That’s the new game.

What Brian Chesky said

Airbnb CEO Brian Chesky was on a podcast saying: as long as you’re a smart person willing to adapt and change, regardless of which direction you go, you’re going to be perfectly fine.

And honestly? That makes a lot of sense. We have these tools that suddenly, in theory, make people more productive and faster. So we’re going to need a lot more people, right?

I want to believe that. I’m not sure I do.

Look — ultimately, I haven’t solved the dilemma of AI being for the rich, by the rich.

It’s depressing, man. I’m not going to sit here and try to come up with some comforting tale about how this can all play out in a way that would be better for all of us.

Not only is AI not getting cheaper — I don’t see an incentive for it to be cheaper.

So all I know is the turtleneck. You put on the turtleneck. You forward-deploy yourself.

I was joking before.

I’m not joking now.
