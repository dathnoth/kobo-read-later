How I grew my YouTube channel from 0 to 140,000 subscribers in 5 months

I grew my channel from 0 to 140,000 subscribers in five months and I think you can do the same.

The key ingredients are content format, packaging, testing, and analytics.

Content format

Humans are hard-wired to listen for stories. A story catches our attention and keeps it until the tension it introduces in the beginning is resolved. On YouTube you won’t have much time to get to the core tension: you have to do so in the first 30 seconds. If you’re able to establish that core premise/tension in the first 30 seconds, most people will stick around until the tension is resolved.

Tension/release is a description borrowed from music, where some keys create tension and must resolve before the listener grows uncomfortable. Once a tense note is hit, the listener holds their breath for the release, and once the resolution note is hit, the listener exhales. The result is a satisfying experience.

You want to start videos with a clear story-telling format, that lets the viewer know they have entered into a story.

Avoid non-storied intros:

  • “Hey guys, in this video I’m going to…”

  • “This video will be about…”

  • “What is up, this is Max here from XYZ Channel, and today…”

Do storied intros:

  • “So the other day, I was…”

  • “So there’s this guy, he’s 42 years old, and…”

  • “I was feeling pretty hungry the other day so I…”

  • “One morning two weeks ago, on a sunny day…”

Do you see how the storied intros immediately create a tension that hooks viewer curiosity? They’ll stick around to find out more about what exactly happened (which you want to artfully delay as much as possible).

You don’t need to overthink the format of a story, like trying to design or engineer tension/release cycles. Stories naturally do that for you. Simply by telling a story you create these structures unwittingly.

It’s possible to make almost anything into a story. Take cookbooks and recipe websites for example. It’s cliche by now that recipes (clearly the thing the user is there for) tend to break into an (annoying) story about how so-and-so recipe is their childhood favorite and it reminds them of their grandma’s favorite perfume or the day their dad finally played catch with them.

You can do this with tech review videos too, for example. You can tell a story about how the device came to be, the founders behind it, any controversies, how you heard about it, how it made you feel, that you had to wait in line for three hours to get it, etc.

If you can start a video with a story, you’ve solidified one of the most important metric in all of YouTube: how many viewers are still watching at the 30-second mark.

The remainder of the video is slightly less formulaic than the first thirty seconds, and largely depends on the kind of content you’re making. What I can say is that you want to keep it super tight, dense, and fast-paced. Cut aggressively. I believe most videos can be made into a 7-8 minute video. You’ll want to get your average view duration (AVD) to at least 50% (more on that further in the article).

Packaging

More important than a good video is good packaging, since a good video with poor packaging will be seen by nobody. Packaging is the video’s title and thumbnail, both equally important.

Titles are more an art than a science I believe, and they can be one of the most annoying parts of the job. But there is consistency to good titles.

Good titles:

  • Tease but do not give away the entire video

  • Are relevant to something happening in the world today

  • Create curiosity around a relevant topic

  • Describe not what the video is, but why it’s relevant or how people should feel about it

  • Create curiosity, catharsis, or vindication

Bad titles:

  • Give away the video and leave no room for curiosity

  • Are entirely fact-based and neutral and don’t arouse a reaction out of the viewer

For example, let’s say you’re making a reaction video to something a politician Klaus Poutson said.

Good titles might be:

  • “He’s saying stupid things again”

  • “Not again.”

  • “This may be the worst thing he’s ever said.”

  • “I can’t do this anymore.”

Bad titles might be:

  • “Klaus Poutson news event reaction”

  • “Going over what Klaus Poutson said in the news yesterday”

  • “Klaus Poutson said [stupid thing here]

You’ll notice the good titles are missing context as to who “he” is. That’s where the thumbnail comes in.

The thumbnail has one-of-two jobs:

  1. Slightly elucidating the subject of the title

  2. Adding head-tilting context to the title

In the case of titles which are alluding to something but not saying what it is (“He”), the thumbnail’s job would be to show who “He” is—in this case, a portrait of the politician in question (perhaps making an awkward face).

If your title instead gives most of the subject away (“Google just admitted AI is impossible”—a decent title, for the record, since ‘impossible’ creates confusion/curiosity), then the purpose of the thumbnail is not to show a Google logo, since that adds nothing. It would be to invert the user’s comfort. Given they’ve read the title and feel adequately satisfied they know what the video will be about, the thumbnail’s job now is to pull out that comfort and make them question what they think they know.

This is admittedly very difficult to do right and you won’t always get it. This is why A/B testing is crucial (we’ll get to that shortly).

In the case of the Google video, you’d want a thumbnail caption (1-2 words at most, in my experience) that creates tension and contrast, or even partial confusion. For example, for a video I had titled “Microsoft accidentally told the truth about AI”, the thumbnail caption I used was “sniper” (because the word sniper came up in the video). In this case, the word sniper creates discomfort and curiosity for a video that should otherwise be about AI and tech. It’s somewhat out-of-place and creates a head-tilt that subverts the expectations a viewer would have about this video.

Again—this process is somewhat hard to distill and my example is not something you should try to reverse-engineer. In my experience the main course is the title itself, and the thumbnail caption is just a little bit of salt on top of the meal to add flavor. To find out what really works, you’ll need to A/B test, which is the most important part of creating and distributing videos.

Testing

Your job as a YouTuber is to channel the zeitgeist (or “meta”) about what audiences are responding to or clicking on now. These tend to take on patterns. Importantly, it’s impossible to try to sniff this out without running experiments. An A/B test simply means you are testing 2 (or 3) variants of a title/thumbnail package for a video when you hit publish (or after).

Instead of coming up with one title for your video, always come up with two, where, importantly, the two titles are very different from one another. They cannot be minor variations on the same title (these will yield inconsequential results).

The best time to run an A/B test is during video launch, since you’ll get results the quickest that way. Each channel is different, but if you have sufficient click volume, you should see results within 45 minutes to an hour or so. If you have little volume, it might take a close to a full day.

A/B tests can make or break the success of your video. Many times I’ve launched videos with a title that I thought was clearly superior, but chose a backup title/thumbnail just in case. In these cases the backup title ended up performing 20% better (which makes a huge difference).

As you run more and more tests, you’ll begin to get an idea for what makes viewers click, especially for the niche you’re in. It’s why giving generic title advice doesn’t always work: each topic caters to different audiences/ages that may respond differently. Your job is to figure out what that is for your niche, and that simply takes experimentation.

Analytics

There are 3 important metrics, the last of which I have surprisingly not seen discussed anywhere, that determines whether your video is a hit or a flop.

The most important metric is CTR—click-through rate: for every 100 impressions, how many click the video? It depends on the niche and the video’s age, but something like 5-10% is pretty good.

The second most important metric is AVD—average watch duration: how long on average viewers watched your video for. This stat can be somewhat brutal, since people who only watch 20 seconds of your ten-minute video will bring the stat crashing down. The important thing is how this figure compares to the rest of your videos and your niche’s averages. In my niche, I find that 50%+ AVD is a pretty strong signal to the algo.

It’s important to understand that the main metric YouTube optimizes for is total watch minutes on the platform: they want users to spend as much time as possible on the app. Watch minutes is a custom metric and not something exposed to you, but it’s a simple heuristic to understand:

CTR x AVD = total watch minutes.

This demonstrates the importance of CTR. Imagine you have a ten-minute video (10:00) with an absolutely incredible AVD of nine minutes (9:00). That’s 90% AVD (which as far as I know is impossible). But, you would think this means this video is really good and thus YouTube should blast it to everyone on the platform, right? But not so fast. What is the video’s CTR? Because here’s the thing. Let’s say you have 100 potential viewers.

If your CTR is 1%, then out of 100, only 1 clicks, and the total watch minutes on the platform is 1 x 9:00 = 9 minutes of total watch time.

If your CTR is 10%, that’s 90 minutes of total watch time.

Now let’s say you have another 10:00 video with an AVD of only 50% (5 minutes)—this is seemingly a pretty low AVD. But not so fast. What is its CTR? Let’s say you nailed the packaging on this one and CTR is an impressive 20%.

20 clicks x 5 minutes each = 100 minutes of time spent on the platform.

You can see how CTR comes out as the most important metric, and AVD ends up mattering slightly less than you’d expect.

Note that the above calculations are just a proxy and not really a hard-written rule. Ultimately many, many more factors go into whether your video spreads beyond just CTR and AVD (shares, likes, subscribes, comments, etc), but they are very important.

The third most important metric which I have not found discussed anywhere is new vs. returning user CTR. This is a hard metric you can actually see, but only in the Advanced Analytics portion of your dashboard, and only 24-48 hours after the video is posted (no realtime metrics for this one unfortunately).

Now, if your returning user CTR is good, you will get a lot of views from your existing subscriber base and people who are accustomed to watching your channel. In general, your video will perform at the floor of your channel’s historical average. To get a breakout video, there is only one stat that matters:

New viewer CTR.

This is what creates runaway successes and mega-hits. Getting new viewer CTR to be high is one of the trickiest and hardest things to pull off, in my experience. Returning viewer CTR can be as high as 15-16% for me, but only 2-4% for new viewers. 4% is actually pretty good, but for low performing videos I typically land at something like 2.9%, which YouTube is not too eager to push out to beyond my loyal viewerbase.

This stat will help you resolve a tension you will run up against one day where you see a video that has a really high CTR and really high AVD, but low views! You’re like, wth!? Does YouTube hate me? Am I shadowbanned? I almost gave up making videos because I couldn’t understand why a video with high CTR and AVD just wasn’t getting views.

It turns out the CTR and AVD was only high amongst my subscribers, but new viewers weren’t clicking all that much, and the ones that clicked weren’t watching all that much.

For breakaway successes and some of my top videos, I find that new viewer and returning viewer stats are pretty close to each other. If you can pull this off, you’ll get filthy rich. But it’s really, really hard to do, and in my experience only happens by chance. But it’s an important lesson to keep in mind: design your titles and thumbnails to speak not to your existing fanbase (they’ll click anything), but instead for people who have never heard of you or your channel before.

If you can do that, you’ll have a long and successful career ahead of you.
