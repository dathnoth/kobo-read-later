The News

Origin Energy got popped, millions of customers impacted

Another day, another massive Australian company begrudgingly reveals there's been "unauthorised access to and disclosure of some customers' data". This time it's Origin Energy and according to emails the hacker sent around to various media outlets trying to extort Origin, the data they accessed is mostly run of the mill stuff like name, address, date of birth etc - stuff that I'd be surprised isn't already out there on every single Australian already. What makes this breach slightly juicy is that it seems to have everything Origin keeps about a customer, including billing info like being on a hardship plan or if you've got unpaid bills and even employee pinned notes. Now the question is - will Origin be punished appropriate, or will the OAIC use the carrot instead of the stick once again?

Google will let you use a video selfie to login now

There's a new way you can log into Google - a selfie video. Give Google a video of your face then if you are for whatever reason locked out (forgot password, can't find your 2FA device, whatever), Google can ask you to take another video and see if it matches. These video selfies act a second factor of authentication. As dystopian as it sounds, this is probably useful at Google's scale and the amount of difficulty most normal people have with passwords and multi-factor authentication. You and I have three Yubikeys in separate locations, a TOTP app on two phones, a 30 character password in a password manager, with backup codes printed and in a fireproof safe. 99% of Google's users don't even know their password, maybe setup a passkey once but not on the device they're currently using and if they lost their phone get locked out of email, photos, contacts, calendars and more.

USA DoJ trying to jail activist for using GrapheneOS

An activist in the USA is in the shit with the feds because they use a phone running GrapheneOS. The USA's DoJ is trying to use a law that "makes it a crime to destroy property in an effort to prevent it from being seized" against someone is opposed to "Cop City", a massive police training centre - but that's irrelevant reallly. The news (at least to me) is that the USA now considers the fact you're using a phone that doesn't collect a shitload of info about you and your activities, "destroying property" if they're investigating you. That's a dangerous precedent and honestly, something Australia's cops would love to try out. Imagine if the same logic is applied to using an app like Signal? Oh you're not leaving any metadata behind - you're deleting evidence, straight to jail for 100 years!!!

Something I Saw On The Internet

Data centre water usage isn't bad, but also isn't good

The Atlantic has an easy to read piece on the water use of datacentres. It's one of the main issues people against datacentres in the local area bring up and one of the (many) reasons people might feel guilty about their use of LLMs. This article takes a brief, but nuanced look at why datacentres use water in the first place, the different ways they can use more or less water, and the overall impact of water use in specific areas. It's not as simple as "they use too much water, bad!!" or "data centre water use is a bee's dick compared to farming, so shut up about it!!". There's also water pollution impacts, not just water consumption.
