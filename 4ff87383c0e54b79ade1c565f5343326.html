The case against Passion

My life story is best told through a series of passions:

Dropping out of university to build apps, quitting every 9–5 job no more than eighteen months in, and building Standard Notes, a single product which, to my eternal surprise, I managed to stick with for eight years.

The death blow to any endeavor was always the same: I began to feel a certain kind of pain. A restlessness. A sense that this wasn’t the true thing I should be doing. What began as an adrenaline-crazed frenzy of productivity and excitement fizzled into something with about as much excitement as a cup of lukewarm black tea.

The emblem of the culture of my youth was Steve Jobs’s Stanford commencement address: you can’t connect the dots looking forward, only backward. Trust the journey. Trust what is in store for you.

A tale as old as modern time, oozing out of every Hollywood script and Pixar movie.

And yet, it did seem true for me. I describe it as a “pain function”—subjective circumstances that reproducibly cause discomfort. Discomfort that you are compelled to evict yourself from at all costs. Each of us has a slightly different pain function, but it decides our course of action.

Back then, I didn’t call it a pain function.

I called it fate. Whispers of destiny.

I see now it is nothing more than an unreliable evolutionary device.

A trick to get you moving.

Take monogamy, for example:

Wise men know that it is correct. It may not lead to a very sexy life, but it can lead to a good one.

Yet were you to indulge in the whispers of destiny, the beckoning of fate, the burning of passion that stirs the heart of men upon the accidental sight of a beautiful woman (one, of course, other than the one waiting at home)—were you to indulge those whispers, you would be led towards a series of events culminating in your total ruin.

Another one of those tales as old as time.

Passion can be a tool, no doubt. It is indispensable in our youth. It breeds adventurers.

But a life of constant wandering? Of exploring? Of chasing passions? That is a life of lust. Of insatiable longing.

Perhaps everyone discovers this at my age? Career promiscuity in your twenties is chic. In your late thirties? I don’t know, it might be a little… sad.

I know this: every pursuit, every craft, every enterprise I have undertaken that started in passion ended in excruciating apathy.

I see now it is the voice itself that is the source of disillusionment.

Passion is promiscuity’s twin sister.

She must be constrained by wiser voices. The voice that knows that every passionate pursuit eventually loses its luster.

That true passion culminates not in fire but in subtle contentedness—in work you must remind yourself you once loved.

Alas, a cup of tea is about as strong as it all gets.
